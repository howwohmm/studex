# Go-To-Market
### Studex — Working Document

---

## The Starting Point

Zero users. Zero revenue. Ten people who said yes.

That's it. That's the honest starting point.

The ten people — Mruja, Dhvani, Prajval, Sumi, Pranita, Vishal, Srishti, Shraddha, Sumit, Puskar — are the first users. Not beta testers in the abstract sense. People we know, who trust us, who will tell us the truth about whether this is actually useful.

Everything else in this GTM plan is built on top of whether those ten people use it every day.

---

## Phase 0 — Do Things That Don't Scale

The first thing we do is not launch. The first thing we do is sit with those ten people and onboard them personally.

Not send a link. Sit with them. Set up their timetable. Watch them use it. Ask what's confusing. Fix it that day.

This is slow. This is the right kind of slow. The feedback from ten people using the product with a founder watching over their shoulder is worth more than a thousand anonymous downloads. Stripe did this — the Collison brothers would show up at someone's laptop and set them up on the spot. Airbnb went door to door in New York. These weren't tactics they were embarrassed by. They were how they learned what actually worked.

**Goal at the end of Phase 0:** 10 people opening Studex every day without being reminded. Not downloads. Daily opens.

## The CR Strategy

The Class Representative is the most underused distribution channel in Indian college GTM.

Every section has one. Their job is to push information from faculty to 50–80 students. They do this on WhatsApp — which means they're managing a group, handling replies, re-sending things people missed, and generally absorbing chaos that should have a structure.

Studex gives the CR that structure. A place to push announcements. A place to drop files. A section dashboard that keeps things organized. The CR's job gets easier. That's not a feature pitch — that's a real benefit to a real person who currently has a real problem.

One CR = one section. One section = 50–80 students pulled in through a single relationship.

Our approach: we know CRs personally at our beachhead college. We approach them not as users but as collaborators. "We built something that makes your job easier. Help us make it right." We give them ownership. We make them feel like the product was built for them — because in a real sense, it was.

---

## The Beachhead

One college. Our college.

Not because we can't think bigger. Because Facebook started with Harvard. Because density matters more than breadth at this stage. Because we know this college — the timetable structure, the CR culture, the WhatsApp group dynamics, the portal everyone ignores.

We don't move to the next college until the first one works. "Works" means students are opening the app daily without being reminded, churn is low, and at least one or two people have mentioned it to a friend unprompted.

When that happens, we take the same playbook — personal onboarding, CR-first, section by section — to one more college where we know someone. Then another. This is how real distribution compounds.

---

## What We're Not Doing

**No big launch.** Launching in ten publications doesn't create daily habits. Daily habits create daily habits.

**No paid ads.** Not until we understand exactly which user retains. Spending to fill a leaky bucket is wasted money.

**No institutional partnerships yet.** Colleges move slowly. Bottom-up adoption is faster, cheaper, and more durable. If institutions want in later, they can pay for it.

---

## The Only Metric That Matters Right Now

Daily active users who opened the app today.

Not installs. Not registered users. Not session length. The person who opened Studex this morning to check what they have today. That's the unit of success at this stage.

Everything else is noise until that number is real and growing.
