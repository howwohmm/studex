# Risks & Honest Answers
### Studex — Working Document

---

## Why This Section Exists

Any investor or grant committee worth impressing will ask what could go wrong. The worst possible answer is "nothing." That answer doesn't make the idea look bulletproof — it makes the founder look like a fool or a liar.

So here are the real risks, stated plainly, with our honest current thinking on each.

---

## Risk 1: Students Won't Build the Daily Habit

**The risk:** The app gets downloaded, used once or twice, and forgotten. The daily habit never forms.

**Why it's real:** Consumer habit formation is hard. Most apps fail at this. Students have enough going on that a new tool has to earn its place in their morning routine.

**Our current thinking:** The bet is on the Today View — showing today's schedule the moment the app opens. This is the lowest-friction daily trigger we can build. The student doesn't have to do anything except open the app. If this doesn't create the daily habit, nothing else will, and we'll know quickly and cheaply.

---

## Risk 2: The CR Doesn't Adopt

**The risk:** The CR strategy depends on a few key people choosing to onboard their section. If they don't, the section-level adoption doesn't happen.

**Why it's real:** CRs are busy. Adding a new tool is friction, even if it ultimately makes their job easier. We're asking them to trust us and change a behavior.

**Our current thinking:** We approach CRs we know personally, not cold. The pitch is specific: "this makes your job easier." We're not asking them to do us a favor — we're offering them something genuinely useful. If we can't convince people we know, we have a product problem, not a distribution problem.

---

## Risk 3: We Can't Find a Technical Co-Founder

**The risk:** Om can't build this alone. If Prajval or Mruja don't commit, or if no one else comes on board, the MVP doesn't get built.

**Why it's real:** This is the current most critical risk. The product is on paper. Getting it off paper requires someone who can build.

**Our current thinking:** The MVP can be built quickly with AI-assisted tools even by someone who isn't a full-stack developer. The goal for Week 1 is a working prototype, not a production app. We're pursuing this in parallel with finding the right co-founder — whoever joins will be walking into something that already has early users and real signal, which is a better recruiting position than "join us and we'll figure it out."

---

## Risk 4: The Product Turns Out to Be a Nice-to-Have, Not a Need

**The risk:** Students say they want this, but when it's built, usage is sporadic. The problem isn't painful enough to drive daily behavior change.

**Why it's real:** The gap between "yes I'd use that" in a poll and "I opened the app every day this week" is enormous. We haven't crossed that gap yet.

**Our current thinking:** This is exactly what the MVP phase is for. We don't know if this is a must-have until we put it in front of real people and watch them use it (or not). The first ten users are the test. If they're not opening it daily after two weeks, we need to understand why before expanding. The honest answer is: we don't know yet. We're about to find out.

---

## Risk 5: The College Blocks or Discourages It

**The risk:** Colleges don't like unofficial tools that route around their systems. A complaint from a faculty member or administration could create pressure to stop.

**Why it's real:** This has happened to other student tools.

**Our current thinking:** Studex doesn't route around the college — it supplements it. It doesn't replace the portal, it doesn't interfere with official systems, and it gives students more visibility into their own academic life. There's nothing here that a reasonable institution should object to. If a college does push back, that's information about that college, not about the product. We start at our own college where we have the most context and the most goodwill.

---

## What Would Make Us Stop

If after building the MVP and running it with 10 real users for 4 weeks, daily active usage is below 50% of those users — meaning fewer than 5 of the 10 are opening it daily — we treat that as a serious signal to either rebuild the core or reconsider whether this idea is real.

We're not committed to Studex regardless of what the market says. We're committed to finding out if it's real, and being honest about what we find.
